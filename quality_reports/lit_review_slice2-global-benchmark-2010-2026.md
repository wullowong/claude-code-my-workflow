## Summary

Two decades of agricultural-insurance evaluation in developing countries have converged on a consistent story: where binding, uninsured production risk forces farmers into low-return, self-insuring strategies, formal index / weather / area-yield insurance shifts behavior on observable margins — investment rises, riskier-but-higher-yield varieties are planted, fertilizer and other inputs increase, and asset-smoothing improves after shocks. The bedrock evidence comes from RCT-grade work in Ghana (Karlan, Osei, Osei-Akoto & Udry 2014), India (Cole, Giné, Tobacman, Topalova, Townsend & Vickery 2013; Mobarak & Rosenzweig 2014; Cole, Stein & Tobacman 2014), China (Cai 2016; Cai, de Janvry & Sadoulet 2015, 2020 — see Slice 1), Mali / Burkina Faso (Elabed & Carter 2015; Stoeffler, Carter, Guirkinger & Gelade 2022), Kenya (Janzen & Carter 2019; Jensen, Barrett & Mude 2017), Bangladesh (Hill et al. 2019), and Ethiopia (Hill, Hoddinott & Kumar 2013). Two recent Annual Review pieces (Carter, de Janvry, Sadoulet & Sarris 2017; Kramer, Hazell, Alderman, Ceballos, Kumar & Timu 2022) synthesize the field.

What is conspicuously sparse is the **labor-allocation literature.** The global-benchmark evidence on "insurance → labor reallocation" has only a handful of clean entries, and they are mostly *general-equilibrium* treatments (Mobarak & Rosenzweig 2014 on wages; Munshi & Rosenzweig 2016 on migration via caste networks). The single most direct, household-level paper is Sakketa & Kornher (2025) on East-African pastoralists — but it studies on-farm vs. off-farm labor under livestock insurance, not the manual-cultivation-to-managerial-work shift our China project targets. In smallholder crop-insurance settings, household labor allocation has been treated as a control variable, a placeholder for "investment intensity," or a casual side-finding (e.g., increased hired labor, increased machinery use), rather than as the headline outcome.

Where China-PAY work fits: the global benchmark establishes (i) that the insurance → investment margin is real, and (ii) that *general-equilibrium* labor-market spillovers exist (Mobarak & Rosenzweig 2014 — wage volatility increases when cultivators get insured). China-PAY work confirms (i) at the smallholder level (Cai 2016) and adds province-aggregate confirmation of labor-allocation effects (Zheng & Li 2023), but the household-level micro-mechanism — and especially the *services-as-bridge* channel — has no global precedent we have located.

## Wedge assessment — does any global paper preempt our "services-as-bridge" thesis?

**Headline verdict (post-CoVe stress-test): the *services-mediated* wedge survives, but a closer-than-expected technology-mediated competitor exists. Project framing must now distinguish service-mediated from technology-mediated insurance → labor reallocation.**

The independent CoVe stress-test surfaced one paper Slice 1's web search missed:

- **Wang et al. (2025), *PLOS ONE*** — "How does agricultural insurance affect income inequality within farmers? Evidence from Shandong Province, China." Tests three mediators of agricultural insurance on rural household income inequality: land transfer, credit access, and **labor allocation**. Empirically estimates *agricultural insurance → non-agricultural employment ratio* with a coefficient of **+5.33% (p<0.01)** at the household level on a Shandong sample. The mechanism that drives the labor-allocation effect is **technology adoption + on-farm labor savings** — i.e., insurance enables technology adoption, which raises on-farm labor productivity, which frees labor for off-farm work. **The services-as-bridge mediator (mechanization-rental markets, custom hire, agricultural production trusteeship 农业生产托管) is absent from the chain.** Outcome variable is income inequality, not labor reallocation per se.

This is the closest extant English-language paper to our project. **Implications for project framing:**

- The narrow claim — "first paper to estimate an insurance → *services* → labor reallocation chain at the household level" — survives.
- The broad claim — "first paper to estimate insurance → labor reallocation at the household level in China" — does **not** survive. Wang et al. (2025) gets there first via a different mediator.
- The project must now explicitly separate **service-mediated** from **technology-mediated** labor reorganization, and present the services channel as a distinct, complementary mechanism — not a substitute for what Wang et al. estimate.

We also searched aggressively (14 distinct query phrasings, NEUDC 2024–2025 program lists, BREAD/IFPRI/J-PAL/IPA working-paper indices) for any global paper that estimates a chain of the form

> insurance → use of specialized service providers / outsourcing / hired-machinery markets → reorganization of household labor

and found **none**. The literature splits cleanly into three non-overlapping camps:

1. **Insurance → input-margin outcomes** — treats hired labor and machinery as input-margin outcomes, not as channels to broader labor restructuring (Cha et al. 2024 on Chinese pear farmers; Stoeffler et al. 2022 on Burkina Faso cotton farmers, who diversify *into* sesame and livestock but not via service markets).
2. **Mechanization / custom-hire / specialized-service-provider literature** (FAO/CSAM reports; the broader 农业社会化服务 literature) — does not test insurance as a treatment.
3. **Services → labor reallocation** (without insurance) — **Zou & Mishra (2024, *AEPP*)** estimates machinery services → labor reallocation in China at the household level. **This is the second leg of our project's chain, estimated without the upstream insurance leg.** The project's contribution is adding the upstream insurance leg, not inventing the services → labor connection.

The other closest global papers that touch the *labor* margin are summarized in cluster (b) below. None of them runs the services / outsourcing / production-organization channel as the explicit mediator. The two strongest candidates remain:

- **Mobarak & Rosenzweig (2014, NBER WP 19811 / "Risk, Insurance and Wages in General Equilibrium")** — shows that insurance to cultivators raises labor demand in good states and lowers it in bad states (general-equilibrium); insurance to laborers smooths their wage receipts. This is a labor *market* effect, not a household-reorganization-via-services effect. CoVe-confirmed: still NBER WP only as of May 2026, no published-journal version located.
- **Sakketa & Kornher (2025, *J. Agricultural and Applied Economics Association*)** — IBLI in Ethiopia / Kenya induces partial sedentarization and a 12.4 pp rise in crop-cultivation participation, with reallocation between on- and off-farm activities. Mechanism is *production diversification*, not service-mediated outsourcing.

**Recommendation for project framing.** Position the project as the *first* household-level estimate of an insurance → **service-mediated** → labor-reallocation chain, and explicitly distinguish it from:
- Wang et al. (2025) on technology-mediated insurance → labor reallocation in China;
- Zou & Mishra (2024, AEPP) on services → labor reallocation in China without insurance;
- the general-equilibrium wage / labor-demand literature (Mobarak & Rosenzweig 2014; Munshi & Rosenzweig 2016);
- the input-margin / factor-allocation literature (Karlan et al. 2014; Cha et al. 2024);
- the pastoralist on-/off-farm diversification literature (Sakketa & Kornher 2025; Janzen & Carter 2019).

The novelty claim — "first household-panel estimate of an insurance → **services** → labor restructuring chain in a smallholder grain economy, with services as the *primary* analytical bridge" — is sustained in the English-language literature as of May 2026, with the precedent papers above named explicitly. **Confidence (post-CoVe): ~90%, conditional on Slice 3 (Chinese-language services literature) returning no insurance-treated services-mediated paper.** The 10% residual reflects what the stress-test verifier explicitly could not rule out: CNKI Chinese-language papers, NEUDC 2024–2025 unpublished sessions, and very-recent paywalled working papers.

## Cluster (a): Foundational insurance-and-household-decision evaluations

### Karlan, Osei, Osei-Akoto & Udry (2014), *QJE* 129(2): 597–652 — Ghana
*See Slice 1 for full BibTeX entry.* RCT in northern Ghana cross-randomizing cash grants and rainfall index insurance. Shows that uninsured risk — not credit — is the binding constraint on investment; insured farmers significantly increase cultivation intensity and choose riskier varieties. The bedrock paper for "insurance enables productive investment" in a smallholder grain context.

### Cole, Giné, Tobacman, Topalova, Townsend & Vickery (2013), *AEJ:Applied* 5(1): 104–135 — India
*See Slice 1 for full BibTeX entry.* Randomized rainfall-insurance marketing in Andhra Pradesh and Gujarat. Demand is price-sensitive but trust, liquidity, and salience are larger frictions than price. Foundational for understanding why uptake remains stubbornly below subsidy-implied levels even in well-designed markets.

### Cai (2016), *AEJ:Economic Policy* 8(2): 44–88 — China
*See Slice 1 for full BibTeX entry.* Cross-reference for Slice 2 — the China experimental anchor that connects to the international RCT corpus.

### Cai, de Janvry & Sadoulet (2015), *AEJ:Applied* 7(2): 81–108 — China
*See Slice 1 for full BibTeX entry.* Social-network diffusion of insurance knowledge. Cited globally as the canonical demand-side study of information frictions.

### Cai, de Janvry & Sadoulet (2020), *AER* 110(8): 2422–2453 — China
Two-year field experiment defining optimal subsidy schemes for weather insurance to rice producers. Past payouts and subsidy levels jointly determine current take-up; combined with financial education, payout experiences create permanent uptake gains. *Newly cited in Slice 2 — not present in Slice 1's bedrock list.*

### Hill, Hoddinott & Kumar (2013), *Agricultural Economics* 44(4–5): 385–398 — Ethiopia
Willingness-to-pay study using 15-year Ethiopian Rural Household Survey panel (n≈1,400). Educated, wealthier, and more proactive farmers buy first; risk aversion lowers take-up under basis risk; provision through groups raises uptake among female-headed households. Foundational for the "who buys index insurance" question.

### Hill, Kumar, Magnan, Makhija, de Nicola, Spielman & Ward (2019), *J. Development Economics* 136: 1–17 — Bangladesh
Hybrid (parametric + indemnity) index insurance pilot. Documents both ex-ante effects (input intensification) and ex-post effects (consumption smoothing) — one of the cleaner papers establishing that insurance does both jobs simultaneously.

## Cluster (b): Insurance and labor allocation outcomes ⚠ DIRECTLY COMPETING

This cluster is **thinner than expected**. Most "insurance-and-investment" papers report labor as a side effect (hired labor cost, planting intensity) rather than estimate a labor-allocation outcome. The papers below are the ones that explicitly target the labor margin.

### Mobarak & Rosenzweig (2014), NBER WP 19811 — Risk, Insurance and Wages in General Equilibrium — India
**The most important global paper for cluster (b).** Three-state experimental rollout of rainfall insurance to *both* cultivators and landless laborers. Labor demand and equilibrium wages become more rainfall-sensitive when cultivators alone are insured (because they switch to riskier, higher-yield methods); insurance to laborers smooths their realized wages by allowing them to reallocate labor supply across rainfall states. **Implication for our project:** the general-equilibrium wage channel is real and well-identified, but it is *not* the services-as-bridge channel — and our household-level design speaks to a complementary mechanism. [UNVERIFIED: whether this paper has appeared in a refereed journal as of 2026; the NBER WP and IZA versions are widely cited but the published version was not located in our search.]

### Munshi & Rosenzweig (2016), *AER* 106(1): 46–98 — Networks and Misallocation: Insurance, Migration, and the Rural-Urban Wage Gap — India
Caste-based informal-insurance networks substitute for formal insurance and *reduce* permanent migration in India. The structural model implies that small improvements in formal insurance availability would substantially raise migration and reduce spatial labor misallocation. Highly relevant to our project as a **theoretical foundation for the migration / off-farm-labor margin**, with the empirical caveat that the mechanism is informal-insurance substitution rather than a service-market channel.

### Sakketa & Kornher (2025), *J. Agricultural and Applied Economics Association* 4: e70010 — IBLI and pastoralist labor allocation, East Africa
**The single most direct labor-allocation-and-index-insurance paper we located.** IBLI coverage in Ethiopia raises the probability of crop-cultivation participation by ~12.4 pp and non-irrigated land cultivation by ~0.34 ha; reallocates labor from livestock-intensive activities toward more diversified on/off-farm livelihoods. Compares effects to cash-transfer programs in same population. **Important caveat for our project:** the channel is production diversification (livestock → crops, including partial sedentarization), not service-mediated outsourcing of cultivation tasks, so it does not preempt our wedge.

### Bryan, Chowdhury & Mobarak (2014), *Econometrica* 82(5): 1671–1748 — Seasonal migration in Bangladesh
RCT showing that an $8.50 incentive triggers 22% of poor rural households to send seasonal lean-season migrants; effects persist 1 and 3 years after the incentive ends. **Insurance-adjacent rather than insurance-treated**: the conceptual link is that migration is risky and a small risk-mitigating subsidy unlocks a binding investment constraint. Cited as a complementary off-farm-labor-allocation result.

### Stoeffler, Carter, Guirkinger & Gelade (2022), *World Bank Economic Review* 36(1): 114–137 — Burkina Faso cotton
Area-yield index insurance has *no* effect on cotton itself but raises investment in sesame, livestock herding, and field improvements. Suggestive of a labor-reallocation effect via crop-portfolio diversification, but household labor-hour outcomes are not the headline. Useful contrast for our paper.

### Emerick, de Janvry, Sadoulet & Dar (2016), *AER* 106(6): 1537–1561 — Stress-tolerant rice in India (insurance-adjacent)
Risk-reducing technology (Swarna-Sub1 flood-tolerant rice) — *not insurance per se* — crowds in adoption of more labor-intensive planting methods, expands cultivation, and raises fertilizer use. Cited here because the conceptual mechanism is identical to insurance ("downside risk reduction enables ex-ante investment") and the labor-intensity finding is a rare clean estimate.

### Wang et al. (2025), *PLOS ONE* — Agricultural insurance, income inequality, and labor allocation in Shandong, China **(surfaced by CoVe stress-test; missed in Slice 1's web search)**
Tests three mediators (land transfer, credit access, labor allocation) of agricultural insurance on rural household income inequality. **Empirical headline:** insurance → non-agricultural employment ratio at the household level, coefficient +5.33% (p<0.01). Mechanism for the labor channel is **technology adoption + on-farm labor savings**, NOT services. **Project implication:** the closest English-language extant paper on insurance → labor reallocation at the household level in China, but operates through a different mediator. The project's services-mediated wedge survives, but the broader "first paper on insurance → labor allocation in China at the household level" claim does not. Project must distinguish service-mediated from technology-mediated chains in framing. *(See Wedge Assessment section above for the full implications.)*

### Zou & Mishra (2024), *AEPP* — Modernizing smallholder agriculture: machinery services and labor reallocation in China **(surfaced by CoVe stress-test; supports the project's downstream leg)**
Estimates machinery services → labor reallocation in China at the household level. **Insurance is NOT the treatment** — this paper supplies the second leg of the project's chain, while the project supplies the missing upstream insurance leg. Useful citation for the services-mediated mechanism's plausibility. *(Bibliographic detail to be confirmed in Slice 3.)*

## Cluster (c): Insurance × services / outsourcing / production-organization ⚠⚠ PROJECT WEDGE

**0 papers found that estimate the full insurance → services → labor reallocation chain.** This cluster is empty in the global English-language literature as of May 2026, based on the original Slice 2 searches plus an independent CoVe stress-test conducted on 2026-05-07. The closest related work falls into adjacent literatures:

1. **Hired-labor-as-input-margin papers.** Cha et al. (2024, *Frontiers in Sustainable Food Systems*; Slice 1) shows insurance raises hired-labor cost among Chinese pear farmers; Karlan et al. (2014) reports rising hiring in Ghana. In both, hired labor is a *direct* production input, not part of an outsourcing-of-cultivation arrangement that frees household labor for other activities.
2. **Insurance → labor reallocation via technology adoption (NOT services).** Wang et al. (2025, *PLOS ONE*) — surfaced by CoVe stress-test, see cluster (b) and Wedge Assessment section. Insurance → non-agricultural employment ratio at the household level via technology-adoption mediator, on a Shandong sample. **The closest insurance → labor reallocation precedent in China at the household level**, but the mediator is different from the project's services-as-bridge.
3. **Services → labor reallocation (NOT insurance).** Zou & Mishra (2024, *AEPP*) — surfaced by CoVe stress-test. Machinery services → labor reallocation in China at the household level, but insurance is not the treatment. **This paper supplies the second leg of the project's chain.** The project's contribution is bolting on the upstream insurance leg.
4. **Pension insurance → mechanization services (NOT ag insurance, NOT labor reallocation).** Liu et al. (2023, MDPI *Sustainability*) — surfaced by CoVe stress-test. Treatment is New Rural Pension Insurance (a different income/age channel), outcome is mechanization service spending, mediator is off-farm labor transfer (in the wrong direction). Architecturally similar but mechanism-different; project should briefly distinguish.
5. **Machinery-use papers without insurance treatment.** The FAO/CSAM custom-hire and specialized-service-provider literature describes the supply-side emergence of mechanization service markets but does not test insurance as a determinant.
6. **Contract-farming-and-insurance bundling.** GIZ / e-MFP work in Zambia describes value-chain delivery of weather insurance through contract-farming arrangements; this is closer to *insurance-as-input-bundle* than to *insurance-causes-outsourcing*.
7. **General-equilibrium wage / labor-demand papers.** Mobarak & Rosenzweig (2014) speaks to wage dynamics, not service-market participation.
8. **Pastoralist labor-diversification papers.** Sakketa & Kornher (2025) reallocates labor without invoking service markets.

**The narrow gap is real.** Our project's contribution is to test whether PAY induces households to outsource manual cultivation tasks to specialized service providers (托管 / 农业社会化服务) and thereby free family labor for managerial / market-oriented / nonfarm work. **No global paper estimates this specific chain.** The closest related work is Wang et al. (2025) (different mediator) and Zou & Mishra (2024) (no insurance treatment); the project's services-as-bridge wedge sits in the gap between them.

**Caveats CoVe explicitly cannot rule out** (per the stress-test verifier): (i) Chinese-language CNKI / Wanfang papers from 2023–2025 that may estimate the chain in Chinese-only venues — Slice 3 (services literature) should pressure-test; (ii) NEUDC 2024 and 2025 unpublished sessions; (iii) PacDev 2025–2026 and ASSA 2026 session-paper combinations; (iv) very-recent paywalled NBER / BREAD / IFPRI working papers indexed only by metadata. **Confidence the wedge holds: ~90% (revised upward from 85% given CoVe's failure to find a preempting paper despite explicit attempts), conditional on Slice 3 returning no Chinese-language preempting paper.**

## Cluster (d): Related downstream-labor literatures (brief)

- **NREGS-as-insurance-substitute (India).** Gehrke (2019, *World Bank Economic Review* 33(2): 413–435) — NREGS access shifts farmers to riskier, more profitable crops (because a wage backstop substitutes for crop insurance). Dasgupta (2017 and others) — NREGS reduces seasonal out-migration in some settings while raising agricultural wages elsewhere; the channel is partial market closure for landless labor. Relevant comparator for the *substitution* of formal insurance by public-employment guarantees.
- **Janzen & Carter (2019), *AJAE* 101(3): 651–671** — IBLI Kenya consumption-smoothing and asset-protection findings (Slice 1 anchor); essential pastoralist comparator for cluster (b) framing.
- **Jensen, Barrett & Mude (2017), *J. Development Economics* 129** — IBLI-vs-cash-transfer comparison in northern Kenya; both raise household income and asset retention through different channels. Relevant for our framing of insurance as an alternative to direct income support.
- **Cash-transfer literature with insurance complementarities.** Daidone et al. (2019) and the broader Transfer Project corpus (Ethiopia, Zambia, Malawi) — cash transfers in agrarian settings shift labor toward on-farm investment and reduce distress wage labor. Conceptually close to the insurance literature but rarely with explicit insurance-treatment arms. [UNVERIFIED: specific Daidone paper details not extracted; cited as a literature pointer rather than an anchor.]
- **BRAC ultra-poor / asset-transfer programs.** Bandiera, Burgess, Das, Gulesci, Rasul & Sulaiman (2017, *QJE*) — asset-and-skills transfers shift the rural poor from casual wage labor toward self-employment in livestock. Insurance is not the treatment, but the labor-restructuring outcome is the closest spirit-cousin in the literature. [UNVERIFIED: full pagination not re-confirmed.]
- **Mexican Progresa / Oportunidades.** Skoufias & di Maro (2008) and others — conditional cash transfers as an income-smoothing mechanism with downstream labor effects. Not insurance-treated; included only as framing.

## Gaps and Opportunities (cross-Slice 1 + Slice 2 perspective)

1. **No global paper estimates an insurance → services → labor-reallocation chain at the household level.** The wedge survives Slice 2. Our China project, if successful, is the first.
2. **The household-labor-allocation outcome is under-studied in the global insurance literature.** Most papers treat labor as a control variable; only Mobarak & Rosenzweig (2014), Munshi & Rosenzweig (2016), Sakketa & Kornher (2025), and Bryan-Chowdhury-Mobarak (2014) make labor the headline. China-PAY work has the same gap (Slice 1 cluster c).
3. **Service-market emergence is documented but not causally linked to insurance.** The FAO/CSAM custom-hire literature describes how service markets emerge; the insurance literature describes how risk constrains investment. *No paper has put these together*. China is a uniquely good setting because (i) PAY scale is the world's largest, (ii) 农业社会化服务 expansion is contemporaneous, and (iii) CFPS provides household-level labor outcomes.
4. **Heterogeneity by farm size and tenure security is under-explored globally.** The Chinese precedents (Feng/Han/Qiu 2021 in Slice 1) are stronger on the smallholder cut than most international work. Our project's smallholder-by-policy-intensity cut would extend the global literature.
5. **Identification standards have improved.** Modern DiD techniques (Callaway-Sant'Anna; de Chaisemartin-D'Haultfœuille) are absent from the China-PAY corpus and uncommon in the global insurance corpus. Adopting them would mark a methodological contribution.
